01
Demolish the Sandpit
6×8 ft · Backyard · ~$60 · Weekend
Do First
Your wife has been asking for a year. Neighbourhood cats are using it as a litter box. It's growing weeds. There is no reason this still exists. Godspeed.
Step by step
1
Post the sand on Facebook Marketplace
List it as "free play sand / fill sand — you shovel and haul." Add a photo. It will be gone within hours. People love free sand for sandboxes, concrete mixing, leveling ground, drainage projects.
Be upfront that it's been an outdoor pit. People using it for non-sandbox purposes won't care at all.
2
Shovel out as much sand as possible
Get the bulk of it out. You don't need every grain — just get it below the level of the concrete and paver edges. Wheelbarrow into piles for Marketplace pickup.
Leave the last inch or two. Nobody will notice and it's not worth breaking your back over.
3
Check for a liner underneath
Dig down a little and see what's under the sand — bare soil, weed barrier, or gravel drainage layer. If there's a weed barrier, leave it. If there's just bare soil, lay a sheet of landscape fabric before adding topsoil.
4
Fill with topsoil to just below the edges
You need about 5–6 cubic feet of topsoil — one bag of garden soil from Home Depot (~$8). Fill to just below the paver and concrete edge height so the sod sits flush when laid. Rake level.
Water it thoroughly and wait a day for it to settle. Top up any low spots before sodding.
5
Buy sod rolls — 6 rolls total
Each roll is 2×5 ft (10 sq ft). Your pit is 48 sq ft so 5 rolls covers it — get 6 for a little extra. ~$5–7 per roll at Home Depot or Lowe's. Tall fescue to match your lawn.
Buy rolls the same day you plan to lay them. Sod dries out fast sitting on concrete in Sacramento heat.
6
Lay the sod
Lay rolls tight against each other with no gaps. Stagger the seams like brickwork so joins don't line up. Press firmly into the soil. Trim around the concrete and paver edges with a sharp utility knife — the existing borders make this clean and easy.
7
Water immediately and daily for 2 weeks
Water thoroughly right after laying. For the first 14 days water daily to keep the roots moist. After that drop to your normal lawn schedule. Don't mow until it's firmly rooted — tug test: if it lifts, wait.
Lay it in the morning, not midday. Sacramento heat will dry out fresh sod fast if you're not careful.

Remove Brick Pillars & Chain
Front yard · ~$60 · One weekend
Next Up
Those pillars and chain are very "1987 tract home." Removing them immediately modernizes the curb appeal, makes the front yard look larger, and opens up the bed for a proper planting design.
Step by step
1
Unhook and remove the chain
Start here — takes 30 seconds. Unhook both ends and set aside. The yard already looks better. Post it on Marketplace or scrap it.
2
Confirm pillars are not structural
Look up — are they connected to the roof, a pergola, or anything overhead? From the photos these are clearly freestanding decorative columns with no load-bearing role. You're safe to demolish.
If in any doubt, tap the pillar and look at the roofline. A structural column will have a visible connection point above it.
3
Knock down the bricks from the top
Use a cold chisel and hammer to break the mortar joints and pop bricks off one course at a time, working top to bottom. Old decorative mortar is usually brittle and breaks easily. Stack the bricks — old used brick has value and you can sell it or repurpose it.
Wear safety glasses. Old mortar chips fly. Not a great look in the eye.
4
Expose and remove the concrete footing
Dig around the base about 6 inches to expose the concrete footing. For shallow decorative footings like these, the lever method almost always works: screw a scrap 2×4 to any remaining stub close to the ground, place a landscape timber as a fulcrum alongside, and use a long metal pry bar as a lever. Stand on the bar — it pops right out.
If it won't budge, dig a little deeper on one side and use the digging bar as a wedge — it'll fall into the hole you created.
5
Backfill the holes
Fill with topsoil, tamp down firmly in layers, water to settle. Since this will be under mulch or new landscaping it doesn't need to be perfect — just filled and level.
6
Install clean steel edging along the lawn border
With the pillars gone there's nothing defining the bed edge. A simple black steel or aluminum landscape edging strip runs along the lawn line and looks modern and intentional — the opposite of the old brick-and-chain look. Stake it in and you're done.
Black 4-inch steel edging is ~$30 for the whole run. Available at Home Depot in the landscaping aisle. Completely different vibe.
7
Haul or list the debris
Old brick moves fast on Marketplace as free or cheap. If you're renting a dumpster anyway for the gravel project, save it all and do one haul. A half-day dumpster for brick + gravel + old landscape fabric runs ~$150–200.

03
Redo the Front Gravel Bed
Front strip · ~$150–250 · Weekend + planting
After P2
Do this after the pillars are gone since the whole bed layout changes without them. Replace the weed-infested gravel with dark mulch, keep the sago palm, add 2–3 drought-tolerant plants. Completely different curb appeal.
Step by step
1
Spray everything with glyphosate
Hit all weeds in the gravel bed with Roundup. Wait 2 weeks for full kill before doing anything else. Do NOT skip this step — any living roots left under the mulch will push straight through it.
Spray on a calm dry day. Protect the sago palm — don't let overspray hit the fronds.
2
Remove or redistribute the gravel
Option A: rake it to the side yard to top up the gravel there — two birds one stone. Option B: post on Facebook Marketplace "free gravel, you shovel." Option C: shovel into a dumpster if you're already renting one for the brick debris.
If the gravel layer is less than 2 inches thick and you're in a hurry, you can leave it and lay fabric + mulch on top. It actually helps drainage.
3
Trim the sago palm pups
Those fuzzy round offshoots at the base need to come off — they cause the messy tangled look. Wear gloves (the spines are sharp). Twist and pry pups off with a flat tool or your hands when they're small. Cut back any dead or brown fronds flush to the trunk.
Wear gloves — all parts of a sago palm are toxic. Keep the cuts clean and don't hack into the crown (the growing tip at the center) or you'll kill it.
4
Lay landscape fabric
Cover the entire bed with heavy-duty woven landscape fabric. Cut an X around the sago palm base and fold the edges under. Overlap pieces by at least 6 inches. Secure with landscape staples every 18 inches.
Use the heavy contractor-grade fabric, not the thin stuff. The thin black fabric tears within a year and weeds laugh at it.
5
Choose your 2–3 companion plants
Plant in fall (October) when Sacramento rains return and temps cool — plants establish much better this way with zero supplemental watering needed. Best options for this bed: Lavender (drought tolerant, purple flowers, great scale), Mexican sage / Salvia leucantha (fall purple blooms, 3 ft wide, almost zero care), or Dwarf rosemary (evergreen, structural, edible bonus).
One plant on each side of the sago palm is enough. Don't overcrowd — the sago will slowly get wider over the years.
6
Add 3 inches of dark wood chip mulch
Cut slits in the landscape fabric for your plants first, then spread mulch 3 inches deep over the entire bed. Keep mulch 3 inches away from the sago trunk — mulch against the caudex causes rot. Natural brown or cedar mulch only — dyed black fades to grey after one Sacramento summer and looks terrible.
7
Apply pre-emergent twice yearly going forward
This is the maintenance step people always skip and then wonder why the weeds come back. Granular pre-emergent in February and again in August keeps the bed almost entirely weed-free. Scatter over the mulch and water in.

04
Laundry Room Countertop + Shelf
Indoors · ~$100–200 · This rainy weekend
This Weekend
Perfect rainy weekend project — 100% indoors. Unstack the LG pair, build a countertop above them at ~39" height, add a shelf above that. Turns a utility closet into an actual laundry room. Folding laundry on top of the dryer is a crime against your back.
Key measurements — LG DLE3400W
📐
Each unit width27" Each unit depth30" Each unit height38¾" Two units side by side54" minimum Countertop sits at~39–40" Countertop depthMatch at 30"
Measure your actual wall space before buying anything — account for the water heater clearance on the right. Add 1–2" overhang on the front edge of the countertop so it looks finished.
Step by step
1
Unstack the machines
These LG units weigh ~122 lbs each — don't do this alone. Get someone to help. Disconnect the dryer power (240V electric — unplug from wall first), then carefully lift the dryer down. Slide both units out slightly to give yourself room to work.
Tape the dryer door shut before moving so it doesn't swing open. Ask me how people learn this the hard way.
2
Paint the walls first
Do this now before the countertop goes in — it's infinitely easier to paint without a shelf in the way. Patch any drywall holes or scuffs first with spackle, let dry, sand smooth. Two coats of your chosen colour. A laundry room is a great place to go bold — dark navy, deep green, terracotta, or a warm white all look sharp in a small utility space.
Use a satin or semi-gloss finish in a laundry room — it wipes clean and handles the humidity better than flat or eggshell.
3
Measure and cut your countertop board
Measure the actual wall width from left wall to the water heater (or wherever you want it to end). Cut your countertop to that width. Standard depth is 30" to match the machines — add a 1–1.5" front overhang so it looks intentional. For material: melamine-faced MDF (~$40) is the easiest and most practical for a laundry room — wipes clean, moisture resistant, no finishing needed. Butcher block (~$80–120) looks great but needs sealing in a humid space.
Home Depot and Lowe's will make one straight cut for free at the store if you bring your measurements. Save yourself the hassle of cutting large sheet goods at home.
4
Build simple side supports
The countertop needs to rest on something — the machines alone won't support it properly since they vibrate. The cleanest method: two simple 2×4 or 2×2 cleats screwed into the side walls at the right height (measure from floor to 39" minus your countertop thickness). Level them carefully — this is the most important step. If your walls are drywall, hit the studs with your screws. Use a stud finder.
Countertop thickness is typically ¾" for MDF/plywood. So if you want the finished surface at 39½", set your cleats at 38¾" (same as machine height) and let the countertop sit flush on top of the machines and cleats.
5
Set and secure the countertop
Lay the countertop onto the cleats and check it's level front to back and side to side. Screw up through the cleats into the underside of the countertop — no visible screws on top. If the countertop butts against the back wall, run a small bead of paintable caulk along the back edge for a clean finish.
6
Add the upper shelf
For the shelf above the countertop, the simplest approach is floating shelf brackets screwed into wall studs, with a matching board on top. Standard shelf height above a counter is 18–24" — enough clearance to stand a detergent bottle upright. Depth of 12–16" is plenty for a laundry shelf (you don't need it as deep as the counter). Make sure you hit studs — laundry products are heavy.
Two heavy-duty floating shelf brackets at ~$8 each from HD, plus a piece of 1×12 board cut to width. Paint the board to match the countertop or walls. Total shelf cost: ~$25–35.
7
Slide machines back in and reconnect
Slide washer and dryer back into position side by side. Reconnect the dryer power cord (plug back into the 240V outlet). Reconnect washer water hoses — check for leaks. Level both machines using the adjustable feet underneath — front-load washers vibrate badly if not level.
Run a short wash cycle and stand there for the spin — if it walks or shakes violently, adjust the feet. A level machine is a quiet machine.
8
Finish and trim (optional but worth it)
Add iron-on edge banding to any exposed plywood/MDF edges for a finished look (~$8 at HD). Caulk the gap between countertop and wall at the back. Add a power strip under the shelf or on the wall for dryer accessories. Done — you now have a proper laundry room.
A small hook rail on the wall next to the machines for hangers is a $15 addition that will get used every single day.
